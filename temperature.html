What is temperature?

Chapter 1 of 15
Chapter 1 — the mystery

Two marbles. One hot, one cold. What's actually different?

Same metal. Same size. Same weight.

Imagine you have two metal marbles. They look identical. Same metal, same size, same weight. You couldn't tell them apart by looking.

But one is hot and one is cold. Touch them and you can tell instantly.

So something is different inside them. Something invisible. It's not the size, not the weight, not the metal. It's some other thing.

That thing has a name: temperature. And the rest of these chapters are about what it actually is.

Chapter 2 — your hand is a liar

Before we measure temperature, your fingers can't be trusted.

Here's a famous experiment with three bowls of water — cold, lukewarm, hot. Try it at home some time.

Same lukewarm water. One hand says "warm!" The other says "cool!" Two readings, one bowl.

Your hand isn't actually measuring temperature. It's measuring how much heat is rushing in or out of your skin right now. That depends on what your hand was just doing.

If you want to know how hot something really is, you need a tool that won't lie. That's what a thermometer is for.

Chapter 3 — the clues

Heat almost anything, and something about it changes.

Long before anyone knew what temperature really is, people noticed: when stuff gets hotter, something about it changes — and you can use that change as a measurement.

Here are six different clues people noticed:

Air balloon
Warm air takes more room. Balloons grow.
Mercury rises
Liquid in a tube climbs when warmed.
Bimetal bends
Two metals stuck together — one stretches more.
Resistance shifts
Electricity flows differently through hot wire.
Hot things glow
A red-hot poker. The sun. They give off light.
Color changes
Some crystals shift color at known temps.

For 400 years, people picked one of these clues and built thermometers around it. Nobody knew why heat made stuff change. They just knew it did, reliably enough to measure.

Let's pick the simplest one — mercury rising — and follow it.

Chapter 6 — other people, other anchors

Fahrenheit picked different points. Same physical thing, different ruler.

About 18 years before Celsius, a German named Daniel Fahrenheit picked his own anchors:

  • 0 °F — the coldest mixture he could reliably make in his lab (ice + salt + water).
  • 96 °F — roughly the temperature of a healthy human body.

Different anchors, different numbers. But it's the same column of mercury. The same physical thing. He just wrote different labels on the side.

Drag the slider. Notice both numbers move together — they're reading the same mercury column. Two scales, one measurement.

So now we have two ways to write a temperature down. How do you turn one into the other?

Chapter 8 — zoom in

For 400 years no one knew why the column rises. Now we zoom inside the mercury.

280

That box is what's inside the mercury, magnified about a billion times. Stuff is made of tiny particles called atoms, and they're never sitting still. They bounce around, all the time, in every direction.

Drag the slider. Hotter = the atoms wiggle harder. Cooler = they wiggle softer. That's it. That's what hot and cold actually are.

How do we know atoms exist? In 1827, a botanist named Robert Brown looked at pollen grains floating in water through a microscope. The grains were jiggling — pushed around by something invisible. That something was the water atoms, slamming into them. Atoms aren't a guess. We've literally watched them push pollen around for 200 years.
Chapter 9 — the miracle

The wiggle inside is what makes the column outside rise.

On the left: atoms in the mercury, wiggling. On the right: the column.

Drag the slider. They move together. That's not a coincidence. It's the same thing.

Hotter atoms wiggle harder. Wiggling harder means they push each other further apart. The mercury takes more space. The column rises.

This is the miracle: every clue from Chapter 3 — the balloon, the bimetal, the resistance, the glow — they all work for the same hidden reason. It's atoms wiggling. The whole world of thermometers, every one we've ever built, is just a way of peeking at the wiggle.

Feynman's bouncing ball. A ball bounces, then slows down, then "stops." Where did the motion go? It didn't disappear. It went into the floor — the floor's atoms started wiggling a tiny bit harder. The floor got slightly warmer. Heat is just motion you can't see anymore.
Chapter 10 — the noisy crowd

Particles fly in every direction. Direction is not the temperature.

particle
wiggle ring (bigger = faster)
average direction

Watch the box. Around each particle is a faint ring — that's its wiggle, a number that says how energetically it's moving. Bigger ring = faster particle.

Now look at the tiny red arrow in the middle. That's the average direction of all the particles combined. Notice anything?

It's basically zero. It barely points anywhere. That's because some particles go left and some go right, some up, some down — when you add them all up, the directions mostly cancel out.

So direction can't be temperature. What matters is the wiggle — how energetic each particle is, regardless of which way it's pointed.

Now we just need to combine all those wiggles into one number.

Chapter 11 — taking the average

One number for the whole crowd. Add them all up. Divide by how many.

Here are 5 particles. Each has its own wiggle level — a number from 1 to 10:

Particle 1 — wiggle =3
Particle 2 — wiggle =5
Particle 3 — wiggle =2
Particle 4 — wiggle =7
Particle 5 — wiggle =3
Add them up: 3 + 5 + 2 + 7 + 3 = 20
Divide by 5: 20 ÷ 5 = 4 ← this is the average wiggle. This is the temperature.

Drag the slider. Every particle's wiggle goes up. The average climbs. That climbing average is what a thermometer reads.

Mercury doesn't measure individual atoms — there are billions of billions of them. It measures the collective effect of their average wiggle: the column rising. That's the trick.

Chapter 12 — heat is not temperature

A swimming pool has more heat than a teacup of boiling water.

Pool: more total heat. Cup: higher temperature.

Which has more heat — the pool or the cup?

The pool. Way more. There are trillions more atoms in the pool, all wiggling. Add up all that wiggle energy and the total dwarfs the cup.

But which is hotter? The cup. Each atom in the cup is wiggling much harder than each atom in the pool. The cup has higher average wiggle.

So:

  • Temperature = the average wiggle per atom. Per particle.
  • Heat = the total wiggle energy in the whole bunch. Adds up.

And here's a rule that matters: heat always flows from hotter to cooler — never the reverse. Drop an ice cube into hot tea, the tea's heat flows into the ice (not the other way). The cube melts because hot atoms keep colliding with the ice atoms and passing on their wiggle.

"Don't let the cold in!" is a fib your parents tell you. Cold doesn't flow. Heat flows out. Same thing in the end, but worth knowing.
Chapter 13 — Kelvin: the honest scale

Drag the wiggle all the way down. The atoms freeze. Right there is zero Kelvin.

280 K

Slide it all the way to the left. The particles stop. There's no wiggle left. That's it. Below this is impossible — there's no such thing as "less than no motion."

This bottom of the universe sits at −273 °C on the Celsius scale. (And −460 °F on Fahrenheit.) It's a real place. But on those scales it just looks like a weird, ugly negative number.

So Lord Kelvin (a Scottish physicist, 1848) said: let's just start the scale there. Call no-wiggle 0 K. The size of each step stays the same as a Celsius degree, so:

K = C + 273
Just shift Celsius down by 273. No stretching. The simplest rule.

Why bother? Because Kelvin is the only scale where the number on the thermometer is honestly proportional to the amount of wiggle. Double the Kelvin, double the wiggle energy. That's why scientists use it.

Bonus chapter

How we actually measure it: 5 different physical tricks.

Every thermometer ever made works on one of five principles. Each one is a different way the wiggle of atoms gives itself away.

1
Things grow when hot
Principle: thermal expansion
When atoms wiggle harder, they push each other further apart. Stuff takes up more room. We measure how much it grew.
Where you'll see it: mercury or alcohol thermometers (liquid in a glass tube), Galileo's air thermoscope (gas pushing water down), bimetallic strips that bend (old wall thermostats, oven dials).
2
Electricity flows differently when hot
Principle: electrical resistance changes with temperature
When atoms in a wire wiggle harder, they get in the way of electrons trying to flow through. Resistance goes up (in metals) or down (in semiconductors). We push a tiny known current through and measure how hard it is to get across.
Where you'll see it: RTDs (platinum wire — used in scientific labs and industry for high precision), thermistors (semiconductor beads — inside your digital fever thermometer, your home thermostat, your phone's battery sensor).
3
Two metals together make a tiny voltage
Principle: thermoelectric effect (Seebeck, 1821)
Weld a wire of one metal to a wire of another, and the joined point produces a tiny voltage that depends on its temperature. No batteries needed — the temperature itself makes the voltage. Really useful for extreme temperatures where other thermometers melt.
Where you'll see it: thermocouples — inside jet engines, kilns, blast furnaces, rocket exhausts. Industrial workhorses.
4
Hot things glow
Principle: thermal radiation (everything glows; hotter = brighter and bluer)
Wiggling atoms shake their electrons, and shaking electrons send out light. All matter does this. At room temperature, the light is invisible (infrared). At higher temperatures, things start glowing red, then orange, then white-hot. We point a sensor at the object and read how brightly it glows.
Where you'll see it: infrared "guns" pointed at foreheads or food, pyrometers in steel mills, telescopes measuring stars (we know the sun's surface is 5,778 K because of how it glows). The only way to measure things you can't touch.
5
Stuff transforms at a known temperature
Principle: phase or color change at a fixed temperature
Some materials flip between two states — solid to liquid, or one color to another — at a precise, known temperature. They can't tell you a continuous reading, but they can tell you "above" or "below" that line.
Where you'll see it: liquid-crystal forehead strips (different crystals turn green at different temps), mood rings, "Tempil" wax crayons that welders draw on metal to check it crossed a heat threshold.

Notice what's happening: each one is a different macroscopic side-effect of the same microscopic motion. Atoms wiggle harder → things grow / electricity slows / voltages appear / light comes off / phases switch.

The way we measure temperature reveals what temperature is. There's only one underlying thing — wiggle — and many different windows onto it.
