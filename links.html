Further reading

Kindred explorable explanations and the lineage they come from. The sibling project worth studying first, the canonical design references, and the curricula that share the conceptual scaffolding behind What is temperature?

Start here
Many Tiny Things — Pontus Granström
A series of explorables explicitly about how macroscopic phenomena emerge from simple microscopic particles — the same pedagogical bet Chapter 9 makes. The companion 2016 Chalmers master's thesis, Explorable Explanations on Statistical Physics, is worth reading for the design rationale; it cites and builds on the Bret Victor / Nicky Case tradition. The closest peer project, and the single thing to study most carefully.

The canon — explorable-explanation design

Bret Victor — worrydream.com
Up and Down the Ladder of Abstraction (and the other essays here) defined the genre this site belongs to: scrub-bar interactivity, multiple linked representations, and trusting the reader to discover patterns. Reads like the design spec for what's already here.
Explorable Explanations — explorabl.es — curated by Nicky Case
The curated index of the form. The Physics tag in particular is a goldmine. Nicky Case's own work — Parable of the Polygons (with Vi Hart) and Fireflies — sets the gold standard for "one tight pedagogical idea, made inevitable through interaction."

For the kinetic-theory chapters (8–11)

PhET Interactive Simulations — phet.colorado.edu — University of Colorado
States of Matter: Basics and Gas Properties are the most-used, most-evaluated interactive physics simulations in the world. They cover exactly the molecular-wiggle picture, with real molecular dynamics under the hood — pump in heat, watch a phase change emerge. They lack the narrative spine here, but they're the empirical benchmark for "does this teaching work?"
Falstad applets — falstad.com/mathphysics.html
The thermodynamics and molecular-dynamics applets in particular. Less polished than PhET, more flexible — and great for the bonus chapter's "five different physical tricks" framing.

Same conceptual moves, different topics

To Build a Better Ballot — Lee Byron & Nicky Case
Voting systems made tactile. Same family of techniques applied to a totally different problem.
Earth Primer — Chaim Gingold (iPad)
Geology as a sandbox. The closest analogue to the "drag the slider, watch the system" aesthetic of these pages.
Distill — distill.pub
Adopted the explorable form for technical machine-learning research. Proof that the genre scales beyond pedagogy into primary research communication.
3Blue1Brown — Grant Sanderson
Interactive math videos. Outside the strict explorable canon, but in the same lineage.
Matt Henderson — simulations on Twitter / X
Short-form physical and geometric simulations. A different tempo, but the same trust in animation as explanation.

Heat & temperature pedagogy (text and curriculum)

The Feynman Lectures on Physics, Vol. I — Chapters 1 and 39–46
The "bouncing ball" aside in Chapter 9 of this site is a direct descendant. Feynman is the conceptual ancestor of the whole "heat is just hidden motion" framing.
American Chemical Society — Heat, Temperature, and Conduction
A middle-school chemistry unit at acs.org/middleschoolchemistry. Lacks the interactive depth but nails the conceptual scaffolding — every demo earns its place.
Eugenia Etkina — ISLE physics curriculum
Investigative Science Learning Environment. Uses the same "notice a clue → build a measurement → explain the mechanism" arc this site does. The pedagogical theory underneath.
If you only read one: Granström's Many Tiny Things, with the thesis open alongside. It's the only project that targets statistical-physics intuition in this exact form, and reading it as a peer will probably yield the most useful design feedback for the next chapters here.
